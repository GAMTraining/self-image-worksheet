Why This Actually Works

Turns out there's a secret to visualization that most people miss. Holmes and Collins cracked the code in 2001. They discovered that unstructured visualization does almost nothing, but when you build it systematically? That's when your brain starts treating practice and reality the same way.

Most visualization is just wishful thinking in HD. LIFT and SHIFT fix that by giving your brain the structure it needs to actually rewire your self-image instead of just daydreaming about being better.

This follows the Let Go, Interrupt, Form, Trust phases to build a mental picture that sticks. Then SHIFT tests it under real pressure, so it's there when the lights are on.

Step 1: Let Go

Dump the Mental Garbage

Before you build anything new, you need to clear out the clutter. What's the mental baggage or negative thoughts that show up under pressure?

What's that one thought that shows up and ruins everything?
How does it mess with your body? (tight chest, shaky hands, heavy legs)
Who are the people who make you doubt yourself? Where do you feel that doubt?
Visualizations that work: Close your eyes. Take three slow breaths, then pick your poison:
  • Watch that tension melt, drain, or evaporate from your body
  • See a color that represents the stress. Watch it drain down your body, out your feet, into the ground
  • Put those doubting voices in a T-shirt cannon and launch them into the sky (or if you're not into that, seeing them get sucked into bubbles and float away works just as well)

Step 1.5: Name Your Current Self-Image

Before you build the new version, get brutally honest about the old one.

When pressure hits, who do you become?
(Examples: "The one who chokes," "The perfectionist who freezes," "The player who fades")
What's the story you tell yourself about why you fail in that moment?
If your current self-image were a movie character, who would it be and why?

Step 2: Interrupt

Hit the Reset Button

You've spotted the spiral (or negative loop). Now break it. Use a quick reset cue or anchor phrase that snaps you back.

What's your reset cue?
(Examples: "Reset," wrist shake, deep exhale, Stop sign, Yellow traffic light)
Can't think of one? Try remembering a time you felt calm and in control. What helped you reset then?

Or steal one of these:

  • "I've got this"
  • "Here we go"
  • "Stay with it"
  • "I'm owning this moment"
  • "Awareness is half the battle"
  • "There's always a way forward"
Visualizations that work: Close your eyes. Take three slow breaths
  • Now see it. See that phrase go into your body and expand it
  • Next, see your muscles tense up and relax
  • Lastly, put your attention on the back of your knees, your right earlobe, your pinky toenail, your elbow. Breathe
Practice this: Use that cue and visualization every time your mind starts spiraling.
Pro tip: If you think you notice everything around you, watch the Moonwalking Bear. Spoiler alert: you don't.

Step 3: Form

Build Your Mental Highlight Reel

Time to create the version of yourself that owns the moment.

Visualizations that work: Close your eyes. Take three slow breaths:
Picture your favorite version of yourself. What does that look like? Who's around? What's your mood? Your body language?

Now drop that version into your pressure moment (competition, presentation, big meeting).

Picture that exact moment: the final program, the qualifying run, the keynote, the takeoff. What do you see?
How does your body feel?
What do you hear?

Visualize this 2-3 times daily. Use every sense you've got. This builds the neural highways that make it real (thanks, brain science). More specifically, Decety and Jeannerod (1995).

Step 4: Trust

Stop Overthinking It

Time to shift from analysis to instinct. Trust what you've trained.

How do you know when you're overthinking? (second-guessing, spiraling, tight shoulders, shallow breathing, shaky legs, racing heart)
Here's what works: Close your eyes. Take three slow breaths
What does trusting your training feel like?
What do you look like when you trust yourself and your skills?
See yourself talking to your coach, friend, parent, mentor, and hear their encouraging words to you. What worked? What didn't?

Now pair your visualization with proof you're ready. Maybe that's:

  • Notes on how each practice felt
  • What's working in your mental prep
  • A quick body pep talk when nerves hit
  • A hug, a dance party or a joke from a friend

SHIFT: Where the Magic Happens

This is where your new mental picture gets battle-tested under real pressure.

Step 5: Add the Chaos

Find somewhere distracting, like a crowded lobby, noisy coffee shop, or a busy hallway. Run your visualization there. When your attention wanders (it will), just bring it back. You're training your focus muscle.

Pro Tip: Yes, this is going to feel awkward, embarrassing or vulnerable. That's the point. You have to train through those moments.

Step 6: Take It Live

Bring it into training and competition. Notice how you handle the nerves and curveballs. Adjust as needed. This is where performance actually changes.

Take notes. What actually worked? Did anything work? Did something else completely throw you off? (Remember, everything is data. Mistakes. Successes. Progress. All data.)
Pro Tip: Can't picture the venue? Google it. Look at photos, then close your eyes and drop yourself into that scene.

Time to Put Your Money Where Your Mind Is

Use this before your next moment of truth. Remember, practice in calm conditions and then bring it to the unpredictable before your event, competition, keynote.

Your New Story

Looking back at Step 1.5, what was your old self-image character?
Based on all your visualization work, who's your new character? What do you call this version of yourself?
Write your new story in one sentence: "I am the person who..."
When pressure hits next time, which version are you choosing to be?
